WEEK 9 · MIDTERM EXAMINATION

Midterm Exam

A 75-minute in-class exam covering Units 1 and 2 (Weeks 1-8). Closed-resource: no laptops, no phones, no AI tools. A single printed cheatsheet is allowed.

What this page tells you: exactly what's on the exam, exactly what isn't, what materials you may bring, and how to prepare.

Format

  • Date: Week 9, regular class meeting
  • Duration: 75 minutes
  • Format: Mix of multiple choice, short answer, and "what does this code print" / "fix this bug" problems
  • Materials allowed: One double-sided 8.5×11 sheet, handwritten or printed, prepared by you. Pen or pencil.
  • Materials NOT allowed: Laptop, phone, smartwatch, textbook, prior labs, AI tools of any kind
  • Weight: 20% of final grade

What's on it

The exam is built from ★ MIDTERM-ELIGIBLE quiz questions across Weeks 1-8. Some questions appear verbatim from those quizzes. Others are variations — same concept, different numbers or context. Study the weekly quizzes and you will recognize what's on the exam.

Topics covered

  1. Week 1 — What is a Computer · hardware vs software, what code is, what an interpreter does
  2. Week 2 — Command Line · navigating directories, running scripts, common commands
  3. Week 3 — Variables & Data Types · int, float, str, bool, type conversion, variable naming
  4. Week 4 — Operators · arithmetic, comparison, logical, operator precedence
  5. Week 5 — Conditionals · if/elif/else, boolean logic, truthy/falsy values
  6. Week 6 — Loops · for, while, range(), break, continue
  7. Week 7 — Functions & Debugging · def, return, parameters, scope, reading errors
  8. Week 8 — Git & GitHub · workflow basics, commit/push/pull, why version control matters

What's NOT on it

  • pandas, DataFrames, plotting (Unit 4 — not yet covered)
  • Dictionaries or list comprehensions (Unit 3 — not yet covered)
  • File I/O beyond conceptual awareness
  • Anything from a Week 9+ topic

How to prepare

  1. Re-do every ★ Midterm-Eligible quiz. They're at the bottom of each Week 1-8 reading page. Aim for 100% without looking.
  2. Re-run your lab code. If your Week 5 lab fails because you forgot how if/elif chains, find out before the exam.
  3. Print the cheatsheets for Unit 1 and Unit 2. Read them. Then prepare YOUR own one-sheet — the act of compressing them onto a single sheet is the studying.
  4. Trace code by hand on paper. Loops on the exam will be small but ask "what does this print." Practice on paper.
  5. Practice reading error messages bottom-up. Several exam questions show an error and ask what caused it.

The day of

  • Arrive 10 minutes early
  • Bring: your cheatsheet, a pen, a pencil, a USF ID
  • Leave: backpack at front of room, phone OFF (not silent), smartwatch off-wrist
  • Bathroom breaks: one at a time, leave phone behind

If something goes wrong

If you have a documented accommodation through USF Student Accessibility Services, you should have already coordinated with the instructor — extra time and the alternate testing room are arranged through SAS, not at the door. If you're sick on exam day, email the instructor BEFORE the exam starts to schedule a makeup. Skipping the exam without notice is a zero.
