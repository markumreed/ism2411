Why this matters

This is the last module of new content — and it is where the previous two modules pay off. You can now load and clean any CSV. Module 15 teaches you to ask aggregate questions and turn the answers into visualizations that communicate to a non-technical audience. These two skills — groupby and plotting — are the most frequently requested in entry-level analyst job descriptions at major employers in retail, finance, and operations.

The business value of aggregation is that it compresses thousands of individual transactions into actionable summaries. A manager does not need to see 5,000 rows; they need to know that the South region generated $420,000 in Q3, that Electronics is the top-selling category, and that revenue dipped 18% in August. groupby() produces those summaries in a single line. Adding .reset_index() converts the result into a clean DataFrame you can plot, export, or pass to another function.

Visualization is the bridge between analysis and decision-making. A table of numbers is analytically complete but cognitively slow to parse. The right chart makes the same information immediately obvious. Choosing the right chart type is itself a skill: a bar chart for comparing categories, a line chart for tracking change over time, a histogram for understanding the shape of a distribution, a scatter plot for exploring correlation between two variables. Module 15 and the capstone will both ask you to justify your chart choices — not just produce them.

Core Concepts

groupby()

Split the DataFrame by a category, apply an aggregation function, and combine the results. df.groupby("region")["revenue"].sum() returns total revenue per region. Chain .reset_index() to get a plain DataFrame back.

.agg()

Apply multiple aggregation functions at once. df.groupby("region").agg({"revenue": "sum", "orders": "count"}) returns a DataFrame with both total revenue and order count per region in one call.

plt.bar() / plt.plot() / plt.hist()

The three workhorses of matplotlib. Always add plt.title(), plt.xlabel(), plt.ylabel() before plt.show(). Save charts with plt.savefig("name.png", dpi=150, bbox_inches="tight").

Seaborn

Built on matplotlib but with cleaner defaults and DataFrame-native API. sns.barplot(data=df, x="region", y="revenue") automatically handles grouping and adds confidence intervals. Great for stats-aware charts with less code.

Chart type selection

Categories → bar chart. Change over time → line chart. Distribution of one variable → histogram. Two variables, potential correlation → scatter plot. Never use a pie chart for more than 4 categories — use a bar chart instead.

Business Scenario

Your manager needs a one-page executive summary before the board meeting Friday. She wants to know: (1) which product categories drove the most revenue, (2) how revenue trended month by month, and (3) whether the order size distribution has changed since last quarter. You have the clean sales CSV from Module 14. Here is the script that produces all three charts:

import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

df = pd.read_csv("data/clean_sales.csv")
df["order_date"] = pd.to_datetime(df["order_date"])
df["month"] = df["order_date"].dt.month
df["revenue"] = df["unit_price"] * df["quantity_sold"]

# ── Chart 1: Revenue by product category (bar) ────────────────
cat_revenue = (df.groupby("product_category")["revenue"]
                 .sum()
                 .sort_values(ascending=False)
                 .reset_index())

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.bar(cat_revenue["product_category"], cat_revenue["revenue"], color="#fbbf24")
plt.title("Total Revenue by Product Category", fontsize=14, fontweight="bold")
plt.xlabel("Product Category")
plt.ylabel("Revenue ($)")
plt.xticks(rotation=30, ha="right")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("charts/revenue_by_category.png", dpi=150)
plt.show()

# ── Chart 2: Monthly revenue trend (line) ─────────────────────
monthly = df.groupby("month")["revenue"].sum().reset_index()

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.plot(monthly["month"], monthly["revenue"], marker="o", color="#60a5fa", linewidth=2)
plt.title("Monthly Revenue Trend", fontsize=14, fontweight="bold")
plt.xlabel("Month")
plt.ylabel("Revenue ($)")
plt.xticks(range(1, 13))
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("charts/monthly_trend.png", dpi=150)
plt.show()

# ── Chart 3: Order size distribution (histogram) ──────────────
plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
plt.hist(df["revenue"], bins=30, color="#34d399", edgecolor="black", alpha=0.8)
plt.title("Order Size Distribution", fontsize=14, fontweight="bold")
plt.xlabel("Revenue per Order ($)")
plt.ylabel("Number of Orders")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("charts/order_distribution.png", dpi=150)
plt.show()

Key Methods Reference

  • df.groupby("col")["val"].sum() — total of val per group
  • df.groupby("col")["val"].mean() — average of val per group
  • df.groupby("col")["val"].count() — row count per group
  • df.groupby("col").agg({"val": "sum", "id": "count"}) — multiple aggregations
  • .reset_index() — convert grouped Series back to DataFrame
  • df["col"].dt.month, .dt.year, .dt.dayofweek — extract date parts
  • plt.figure(figsize=(w,h)) — set figure size in inches
  • plt.bar(x, y) — bar chart
  • plt.plot(x, y, marker="o") — line chart with markers
  • plt.hist(series, bins=20) — histogram
  • plt.title() / plt.xlabel() / plt.ylabel() — required labels
  • plt.savefig("name.png", dpi=150, bbox_inches="tight") — save chart
  • sns.barplot(data=df, x="cat", y="val") — Seaborn bar with confidence intervals
  • sns.histplot(df["col"]) — Seaborn histogram
  • sns.boxplot(data=df, x="cat", y="val") — box plot by category
Common Misconceptions

Pitfall 1 — groupby result is a Series, not a DataFrame: df.groupby("region")["revenue"].sum() returns a Series with the region as the index. To plot it with matplotlib's plt.bar(x, y), you need two arrays. Use .reset_index() to convert it to a DataFrame with named columns.

Pitfall 2 — Charts without labels look unprofessional: A chart with no title, unlabeled axes, and default tick formatting tells the reader nothing about what they are looking at. Always add plt.title(), plt.xlabel(), and plt.ylabel() before plt.show(). In the capstone, unlabeled charts lose points.

Pitfall 3 — Using plt.show() before savefig: Calling plt.show() clears the figure. If you call plt.savefig() after plt.show(), you will save a blank file. Always save before showing: plt.savefig(...); plt.show().

Q8 · Multiple Choice
You want to compute both total revenue AND average order count per region in one call. Which method do you use?
  • a) df.groupby("region")["revenue"].sum().mean()
  • b) df.groupby("region").agg({"revenue": "sum", "order_id": "count"})

Q9 · Code Trace
Why does plt.savefig() need to be called BEFORE plt.show()?
Answer
plt.show() renders and then clears the current figure. If savefig() is called after show(), it saves a blank (empty) figure. Always: plt.savefig("name.png"); plt.show() — save first, show second.
